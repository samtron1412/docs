# Camera

## Summary

- Type:
    + SLT (Analog version) (Single Lens Reflex)
    + DSLR (Digital Single Lens Reflex)
        * It uses a mirror system to reflect light through the lens into
          an optical viewfinder (OVF), allowing the photgrapher to see
          what the lens sees in real-time.
        * When you press the shutter, the mirror mechanism moves out of
          the way, allowing light to hit the digital sensor to capture
          the image.
    + Mirrorless
        * It does not use an optical viewfinder and mirror mechanism to
          reflect light.
        * Instead, light travels directly to the digital sensor from the
          lens, creating the image and displaying it on an electronic
          viewfinder.
- Brands
    + Canon (beginner friendlier than Nikon in term of usability)
    + Nikon (professional focus)
    + Sony

# Lens

- Numbers and Values on Lens
    + Focal Length (e.g., 50mm, 18mm-150mm)
        * It dictates the field of view, or how "zoomed in" the image
          is.
        * Smaller numbers mean wide-angle view: 45 or 50 mm focal point
          has a similar field of view as our eyes.
            * Smaller than 45 is for landscape and larger field of view.
            * Bigger than 50 is for portrait and zoomed in (telephoto
              view) such as wildlife/sport)
        * Zoom (variable focal length) vs Prime (fixed)
    + Aperture (e.g., f/1.8, f/3.5-5.6, 1:2.8)
        * The size of the opening that lets light into the camera.
        * Smaller f-number means a larger opening, allowing more light
          in, which is better for low-light situations.
    + Diameter symbol followed by a number: the filter thread size in
      millimeters, indicating which size filters to buy.
- IS (Image Stabilization): internal technology that reduces blur from
  camera shake.
- USM / STM: autofocus motor
    + USM is fast and precise for still shooting
    + STM is smooth and quiet, ideal for video

# Memory Cards

## SD (Secure Digital) Cards

- Capacity
    + SD/SDHC/SDXC/SDUC: up to 2GB/32GB(FAT32)/2TB(exFAT)/128TB
    + Standard Capacity / High Capacity / Extended Capacity / Ultra
      Capacity
- Speed Class (Minimum sustained speed)
    + Speed Class (C): the number inside the C indicates speed (C10 = 10
      MB/s)
    + UHS Speed Class (U)
        * U1: minimum 10 MB/s)
    + Video Speed Class (V)
        * V30: 30 MB/s
        * V60/V90: 60-90 MB/s
    + Bus interface speed
        * UHS-I, up to 104 MB/s
        * UHS-II, up to 312 MB/s (uses a second row of pins)
- Application Performance Class (A1/A2): these indicate how well the
  card runs apps (e.g., on smartphones or Nintendo Switch)
    + A1: minimum 1500 read / 500 write IOPS (input/ouput operations per
      second)
    + A2: min 4000 read / 2000 read IOPS.

## CFexpress (Type A or Type B)

- Higher read and write speed than SD cards.
    + Type B: up to 2000 MB/s

# Photo Shooting / Taking

Here is how to optimize your Canon R50 for superior results:
1. Settings for Superior Quality

    Shoot in RAW: Enable RAW or C-RAW+JPEG in the menu. RAW files retain much more data, allowing for better editing in Lightroom, whereas iPhones apply heavy, irreversible computational processing.
    Aperture Priority Mode (Av): Use Av mode to control your depth of field. A lower f-number (e.g., f/2.8) creates a shallow depth of field with blurry backgrounds (bokeh) that phones struggle to replicate naturally.
    ISO Settings: Keep ISO as low as possible (ISO 100-800) for cleaner, less grainy images. Only increase it in low light.
    White Balance: Move away from Auto White Balance (AWB) in tricky lighting. Use presets like Cloudy or Daylight for more accurate colors.

2. Essential Lenses
While the kit lens is capable, it has limitations.

    Prime Lenses: The RF 50mm f/1.8 STM is a very affordable upgrade that offers far better low-light performance and sharper images with superior bokeh compared to the 18-45mm kit lens.
    Fast Zoom Lenses: Third-party options like the Sigma 18-50mm f/2.8 offer a constant bright aperture throughout the zoom range.

3. Composition & Focusing

    Use Whole Area AF: Set the autofocus to "Whole Area AF" to scan the entire frame for subjects, which is ideal for portraits.
    Eye Detection: Enable eye detection to ensure focus is always on the subject's eye, which is critical for portraits, notes this YouTube video.
    Use the Gridlines: Enable the grid in your viewfinder to improve composition.

4. Post-Processing

    Editing RAW Files: Use software like Adobe Lightroom to adjust highlights, shadows, and colors, which will make the image quality far exceed the compressed JPEG output of a smartphone.

## Common Issues

- Motion blur
    + A function of shutter speed and your subject's movement
    + Faster shutter speed will help with capturing moving subjects.
- Subject separation: a function of your depth of field and the distance
  between your subject and the background.
    + A shallower depth of field, then a wider aperture (lower f value:
      1.4 - 4): sharp subject and a blurry background (ideal for
      portraits)
    + High f-number (f/11-22): a narrow aperture with a deep depth of
      field, keeping both the foreground and background in focus (ideal
      for landscpes)

# Photo Editing

Image / photo editing is the process of altering digital or traditional
photographs to enhance, correct, or creatively modify them, ranging from
simple adjustments like lighting and cropping to advanced
manipulation. Key focus areas include improving exposure, correcting
white balance, enhancing colors, and removing distracting elements to
improve the image's story and aesthetic quality.

Key Areas to Focus On in Photo Editing:

- Lighting and Exposure: Adjust the brightness or darkness of the photo, bringing out details in shadows and highlights, and ensuring the contrast is balanced.
- Color Correction & White Balance: Adjust the Temperature (warm/cool) and Tint (green/magenta) to match the scene's actual lighting or create a desired mood.
- Composition and Framing: Use cropping to remove unwanted elements, improve the image's composition, and focus attention on the main subject.
- Correction and Cleaning: Remove, or spot retouch, distracting background objects, blemishes, or dirt to create a cleaner image.
- Enhancing Details: Use sharpening tools to make images clearer, and subtly adjust clarity or texture, ensuring not to overdo it, which can cause unnatural results.

Essential Editing Tips:

- Use a Histogram: Use this to analyze and guide the proper exposure levels of your photo.
- Be Subtle: Overediting, or overusing settings like contrast and saturation, can reduce image quality.
- Set a Goal: Determine what story the photo is trying to tell before beginning the editing process.
- Focus on the Subject: Ensure your edits bring attention to the primary focus of the picture.
